King Henry VIII the guy we all hated

Likely one of the most famous Kings in history is King Henry VIII. Known as a renaissance man, he was one of the most charismatic kings in history. He did a lot while on the throne but is the example of why too much power corrupts. In fact, he not only embarrassed the crown but also embarrassed the family before him. Henry was married to Catherine of Aragon originally, a woman of older note. Henry and Catherine came together in an arranged marriage, so he never truly felt love for her as he and she had hoped. The rub at the time? The Church had far too much power.

At that time, the church did not allow divorce. Once Henry married Catherine, that was the end of that to the church. Even the King himself was bound by the church. This led the King to separate the church from the authority of The Pope. This would take The Pope's power down exponentially. Henry then made himself the "Supreme Head of the Church of England." He was able to get his marriage annulled, and he would go on to marry multiple women. He would do this mostly due to never seemingly having a male heir to the throne with his first few wives.

He would marry five more times. Two of these women would be executed: Ann Boleyn and Catherine Howard. Two others were also annulled (Jane Seymor died after giving birth). Meanwhile, Catherine Parr, his last wife, outlived him. Most of these marriages would last three years or less. He would eventually have multiple children, but only one son. After Henry's passing, his son would succeed him at the throne, becoming Edward VI. Doing so as a boy, around age 10. Yet his reign lasted a mere five and a half years. Richard III And His Paranoia

Yet in 1483, before Edward V could even get the crown, things changed. It was found that his parents' marriage was invalid, being declared "bigamous." This made he and his sibling illegitimate, according to the Church and the Assembly of Lords and commoners. They would recommend the brother, Richard, for the throne instead.

With neither being able to take the throne due to this controversy, the boys saw their uncle Richard III be made King. The following day after he is made King, on the orders of the Richard III himself, his two young nephews(12 and 9) would be taken to the Tower of London. Literally, Richard is responsible for the death of his two young nephews. Neither of which were even eligible for the throne already.
