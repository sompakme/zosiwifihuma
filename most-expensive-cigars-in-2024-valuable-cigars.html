Mayan Sicars

Mayan Sicars are pre-Columbian cigars discovered in Guatemala. These cigars date back 600 years and were found in sealed clay pots, preserving their quality. The Mayan civilization, known for its advancements in agriculture and craftsmanship, cultivated tobacco and rolled it into cigars, creating what is now considered a remarkable historical artifact. These pre-Columbian cigars are a testament to the rich cultural heritage of the Mayans and their affinity for tobacco.

The significance of Mayan Sicars extends beyond their historical value. They offer a glimpse into the rituals, customs, and trade practices of the Mayan people. The tobacco used in these cigars is believed to be derived from rare tobacco plants indigenous to the region, adding to their intrigue and desirability among collectors and cigar enthusiasts.

“Discovering Mayan Sicars was like unveiling a hidden chapter of history. These cigars provide a tangible connection to the past, offering a unique insight into the traditions and craftsmanship of the Mayan civilization.” – Gary Liotta, Collector

The Mayan Sicars gained international attention when collector Gary Liotta acquired a set of these pre-Columbian cigars for a staggering $507,000 at an auction. This acquisition cemented their status as one of the rarest and most valuable cigars in the world.

The Features of Mayan Sicars:

  • Origin: Guatemala
  • Age: 600 years
  • Preservation: Sealed clay pots
  • Tobacco: Rare indigenous tobacco plants
  • Value: $507,000 (acquired by Gary Liotta)
